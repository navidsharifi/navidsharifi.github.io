Research project collaborator at Cardiff University under the supervision of Prof. Bahman Rostami-Tabar.

## Overview

This project examines a central problem in global health logistics: why large investments in immunisation supply chains do not always lead to stronger supply chain performance.

Every year, millions of children miss life-saving vaccines even when those vaccines have already been produced, financed, and distributed into country systems. In many settings, the barrier is not vaccine availability at the global level. The challenge is whether the supply chain can move the right vaccine, in the right quantity, at the right time, while preserving quality across every storage and transport stage.

The research focuses on understanding how resources and operational practices translate into measurable outcomes across the immunisation supply chain. It asks whether facilities that appear well-equipped are also executing the right processes, and whether those processes are actually producing reliable service performance.

## Why This Research Matters

Immunisation supply chains are highly sensitive systems. Vaccines may pass through national stores, regional warehouses, district health offices, and health centres before reaching patients. Across that journey, temperature control, stock accuracy, forecasting, handling, and transport reliability all matter.

When one link in the chain fails, the consequences can be severe:

- vaccines can be exposed to damaging temperatures
- stock-outs can interrupt routine immunisation
- wastage can rise through expiry or mishandling
- service delivery can become unreliable in already vulnerable communities

Despite sustained investment by governments, WHO, UNICEF, Gavi, and other partners, these failures remain common in many low- and middle-income countries. This project aims to explain that gap between investment and performance.

## The EVM Framework

The study uses the Effective Vaccine Management (EVM) framework developed by WHO and UNICEF. EVM is especially valuable because it does not evaluate supply chains through a single pass-fail lens. Instead, it captures three connected dimensions of operational reality:

1. **Inputs**: whether facilities have the required functions, infrastructure, equipment, and resources.
2. **Outputs**: whether staff and systems are carrying out the right operational processes.
3. **Performance**: whether those inputs and processes are producing the intended service outcomes.

This structure makes EVM a strong basis for analysing input-output-performance relationships rather than just reporting isolated compliance scores.
