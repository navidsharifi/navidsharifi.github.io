Understanding Input-Output-Performance Relationships in Immunisation Supply Chain

# Why This Research Matters

Every year, millions of children in low- and middle-income countries miss out on vaccines
that could protect them from measles, polio, tetanus, and other preventable diseases. In
2024, 14.3 million children received no vaccinations at all. Many of these children live
in places where vaccines exist — the problem is not that vaccines were not produced or
purchased. The problem is that the supply chain that was supposed to deliver them to those
children failed.

Immunisation supply chains are complex. A vaccine produced at a factory in Europe or India
must travel through a national store, a regional warehouse, a district health office, and
finally to a health centre where a nurse gives the injection. Along the entire journey, the
vaccine must be kept within a specific temperature range — usually between 2°C and 8°C for
most vaccines, and below −15°C for some. It must arrive before its expiry date. It must be
the right vaccine, in the right quantity, at the right time. If any link in this chain fails,
the vaccine may be wasted, damaged, or simply unavailable when a child needs it.

Governments, international organisations like WHO and UNICEF, and donors like GAVI invest
enormous resources in trying to fix these supply chains. They pay for refrigerators, vehicles,
training programmes, information systems, and management procedures. Despite this investment,
supply chain failures remain stubbornly common. Vaccines are still damaged by heat. Stock-outs
still occur. Forecasts are still inaccurate. Waste is still disposed of unsafely.

This raises a fundamental question: **why does investment in supply chain inputs not reliably
translate into better supply chain performance?**

This research sets out to answer that question.


# The EVM Framework: A Unique Window into Supply Chain Operations

## What EVM Is

The Effective Vaccine Management (EVM) framework is a standardised assessment tool developed
by WHO and UNICEF and deployed in more than 80 countries. It is used to evaluate how well
health facilities — from national stores to small rural health centres — are managing their
immunisation supply chains.

What makes EVM unusual, and what makes it so valuable for research, is that it does not just
ask "is this facility performing well?" It asks three separate questions simultaneously:

1. Does the facility have the right **functions/resources**?
2. Is the facility **doing** the right things with those resources/fucntions?
3. Is the facility **achieving** the right results?

These three questions correspond to three types of requirements in the EVM framework, which
we call **Inputs**, **Outputs**, and **Performance**.
